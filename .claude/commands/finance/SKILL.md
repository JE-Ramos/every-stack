# Finance
> The founder who turns spreadsheets into strategy — owning the numbers that determine whether the company lives, dies, or thrives, from unit economics to fundraising to cash management.

## What Elite Looks Like

An elite founder-level finance operator doesn't just track numbers — they use financial models as a strategic weapon. They know that startups don't die from lack of ideas; they die from running out of money. As Garry Tan's framework emphasizes, financial fluency is one of the six non-negotiable skills for startup founders because every decision — hiring, pricing, fundraising, product investment — is ultimately a financial decision.

What separates elite from competent:
- **Unit economics obsession**: Can recite LTV, CAC, payback period, gross margin, and burn multiple from memory. Knows when these metrics signal real product-market fit vs. subsidized growth
- **Scenario discipline**: Maintains multiple financial scenarios (base, upside, downside, catastrophe) and updates them monthly. Never operates on a single forecast
- **Fundraising as strategy, not desperation**: Raises capital from a position of strength, with a clear plan for how each dollar converts to enterprise value. Understands dilution, control, and optionality
- **Cash flow paranoia**: Knows the exact date the company runs out of money at current burn. Manages runway like oxygen
- **Pricing courage**: Treats pricing as a strategic lever, not a cost-plus calculation. Tests, iterates, and isn't afraid to charge more
- **Board-level communication**: Presents financials as a narrative — here's where we are, here's where we're going, here's what we need — not just a data dump

## Core Responsibilities

- Build and maintain the company's financial model (3-statement model with scenario analysis)
- Track and optimize unit economics: LTV, CAC, gross margin, contribution margin, payback period
- Manage cash flow and runway — maintain at least 18 months of runway at all times if possible
- Lead fundraising: pitch deck, financial narrative, term sheet negotiation, due diligence
- Manage the cap table: equity grants, option pools, dilution modeling, 409A valuations
- Set and manage the company budget: department-level allocation, variance tracking, reforecasting
- Design and iterate on pricing strategy based on willingness-to-pay data and competitive positioning
- Produce monthly financial reports for the leadership team and board
- Manage investor relations: monthly updates, board meeting preparation, ad hoc data requests
- Ensure financial compliance: tax obligations, audit preparation, legal entity structure

## Key Skills & Tools

### Financial Modeling
- 3-statement models (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow) with driver-based assumptions
- SaaS metrics modeling: MRR/ARR waterfall, cohort-based retention, expansion revenue
- Scenario planning: base/upside/downside cases with sensitivity analysis on key drivers
- Bottoms-up revenue forecasting from pipeline data and conversion rates
- Top-down market sizing: TAM/SAM/SOM with defensible methodology

### Unit Economics
| Metric | What It Tells You | Healthy Benchmark (B2B SaaS) |
|--------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| **LTV** | Total value of a customer over their lifetime | >3x CAC |
| **CAC** | Total cost to acquire one customer | Payback <18 months |
| **Gross Margin** | Revenue minus cost of goods sold | >70% for software |
| **Net Revenue Retention** | Expansion minus churn from existing customers | >110% |
| **Burn Multiple** | Net burn / net new ARR | <2x is excellent |
| **Magic Number** | Net new ARR / prior quarter S&M spend | >0.75 is efficient |
| **Rule of 40** | Revenue growth rate + profit margin | >40% is strong |

### Fundraising
- Pitch deck construction: problem, solution, market, traction, team, ask, use of funds
- Term sheet literacy: pre-money vs. post-money, liquidation preferences, pro-rata rights, anti-dilution, board seats, protective provisions
- Cap table management: modeling dilution across rounds, option pool sizing, employee equity
- Due diligence preparation: data room organization, financial audit readiness
- Investor targeting: tier-based outreach, warm intros, signaling risk management

### Cash Flow & Treasury
- 13-week cash flow forecasting for near-term liquidity management
- Accounts receivable and payable optimization
- Banking relationships and credit facilities
- Tax planning: R&D credits, entity structuring, international considerations

## AI-First Practices

AI is becoming a force multiplier for financial operations, especially for resource-constrained startup founders who can't yet afford a full finance team.

### Financial Model Generation & Analysis
- Use Claude/GPT to generate initial financial model templates with SaaS-specific assumptions and formulas
- AI-assisted sensitivity analysis: "What happens to runway if churn increases 2% and we delay the next hire by 3 months?"
- Automated variance analysis: feed actual vs. budget data and get AI-generated explanations for key variances
- Scenario modeling in natural language: describe business changes and have AI translate them into model adjustments

### Fundraising Preparation
- AI-generated first drafts of pitch deck narrative sections (problem framing, market analysis, competitive positioning)
- Term sheet analysis: paste in a term sheet and get AI-generated explanation of each clause, with flags for founder-unfriendly terms
- Investor research: AI-synthesized profiles of potential investors — portfolio, check size, stage preference, thesis alignment
- Due diligence Q&A preparation: AI-generated answers to common due diligence questions based on your financial data

### Market Research & Sizing
- AI-powered TAM/SAM/SOM analysis using public data, industry reports, and analyst estimates
- Competitive pricing intelligence: AI analysis of competitor pricing pages, G2 reviews, and public financial data
- Market trend synthesis from earnings calls, SEC filings, and industry publications

### Reporting & Communication
- AI-generated monthly investor update drafts from raw metrics
- Board deck narrative generation: translate financial data into strategic storytelling
- Automated KPI commentary: flag anomalies and generate explanations for metric movements

## How They Collaborate

- **Sales & Marketing**: Aligns CAC targets with unit economics; validates that growth investments generate positive ROI; sets pricing that balances growth and margin
- **Product**: Provides financial framework for build-vs-buy decisions; models the revenue impact of product investments; ensures roadmap aligns with financial plan
- **Engineering**: Manages engineering headcount budget; evaluates cost of infrastructure and tooling decisions; models technical debt as financial debt
- **Brand**: Budgets for brand investments with longer payback periods; measures brand's impact on CAC reduction and pricing power over time
- **Leadership**: Presents financial health to the board; partners on hiring plan and org design within budget constraints; co-leads fundraising

## Hiring Signals

### Green Flags
- Can build a financial model from scratch in a spreadsheet — no templates, no tools, just logic and assumptions
- Explains unit economics in plain English, connecting the math to business strategy
- Has been through a fundraising process and can discuss what they'd do differently
- Understands the difference between accounting (backward-looking) and finance (forward-looking)
- Shows comfort with ambiguity: can make decisions with 60% of the data and update as they learn
- Asks "what assumptions drive this?" before looking at outputs
- Has experience managing cash in constrained environments — not just big-company budgets

### Red Flags
- Treats financial models as static documents rather than living decision tools
- Can't explain the relationship between growth rate, burn, and runway without a spreadsheet
- Focuses on GAAP metrics for an early-stage company instead of SaaS operating metrics
- "We'll figure out pricing later" — treats pricing as an afterthought
- Fundraising strategy is "raise as much as possible at the highest valuation" with no dilution awareness
- No understanding of cap table mechanics or how equity decisions compound over time
- Can't articulate what "default alive" vs. "default dead" means for their company

## Growth Path

| Level | Markers |
|-------|---------|
| **Founder (Pre-Seed)** | Builds first financial model in Google Sheets. Manages cash personally. Bootstraps or raises friends-and-family round. Sets initial pricing through customer conversations |
| **Founder (Seed)** | Raises institutional capital. Negotiates first term sheet. Establishes unit economics tracking. Manages monthly budget. Sends investor updates. Gets 409A valuation |
| **Head of Finance** | Owns full financial model with scenario planning. Manages fundraising process end-to-end. Establishes FP&A cadence. Implements proper accounting (accrual basis). Manages audit and tax |
| **VP Finance** | Builds finance team (FP&A, accounting, treasury). Implements financial systems and controls. Manages board reporting. Leads Series B+ fundraising. Develops sophisticated pricing strategy |
| **CFO** | Strategic partner to CEO. Owns the financial narrative for all stakeholders. Manages banking relationships, debt facilities, and treasury. Prepares for IPO or exit. Drives M&A financial analysis |

## RTFM — Essential Reading

### Books
- **Venture Deals** — Brad Feld & Jason Mendelson. The definitive guide to startup fundraising mechanics: term sheets, cap tables, negotiation, and the VC ecosystem. Read before your first fundraise
- **Financial Intelligence** — Karen Berman & Joe Knight. Makes accounting and finance accessible for non-finance people. Essential for technical founders who need to build financial fluency
- **The Hard Thing About Hard Things** — Ben Horowitz. Not a finance book per se, but the most honest account of the financial and operational crises founders face. Every chapter has financial decision-making lessons
- **Secrets of Sand Hill Road** — Scott Kupor (Andreessen Horowitz). How venture capital actually works — from fund mechanics to term sheet negotiation to board dynamics
- **The Intelligent Investor** — Benjamin Graham. The foundation of value investing and valuation thinking. Teaches you to think about your company the way sophisticated investors do
- **Pricing Creativity** — Blair Enns. Reframes pricing as a value conversation rather than a cost calculation. Especially relevant for services and premium SaaS
