# UX Researcher

> The researcher who turns ambiguity into evidence — generating actionable insights through rigorous methodology, not just collecting data — so that product decisions are grounded in real human behavior rather than stakeholder assumptions.

## What Elite Looks Like

A staff-level UX researcher doesn't just run studies. They build research programs that systematically reduce the risk of building the wrong thing. They choose methods with the precision of a scientist — knowing exactly when a diary study reveals what an interview cannot, when quantitative data supplements qualitative insight, and when "good enough" research now beats perfect research too late. They synthesize across studies to surface strategic patterns, not just tactical findings.

What separates elite from competent:
- **Methodological rigor**: Selects the right method for the question — doesn't default to usability testing for everything. Understands the strengths, limitations, and biases of each approach
- **Strategic framing**: Connects research questions to business decisions. Every study answers "what will we decide differently based on this?" before it begins
- **Synthesis depth**: Sees patterns across multiple studies, products, and user segments. Builds cumulative knowledge, not isolated findings
- **Stakeholder influence**: Presents findings in ways that change minds and drive action — not 80-slide decks that get filed away. Knows that insight without impact is just overhead
- **Research democratization**: Enables the whole team to do lightweight research while maintaining quality standards — not gatekeeping research as a specialist-only activity
- **Participant empathy**: Builds rapport quickly, asks questions that surface genuine behavior (not socially desirable answers), and creates safe environments for honest feedback
- **Triangulation instinct**: Doesn't trust any single data source. Combines qualitative, quantitative, and behavioral data to build robust understanding

## Core Responsibilities

- Define research strategy aligned with product roadmap and key decision points
- Plan and execute qualitative studies — user interviews, contextual inquiry, diary studies, concept testing
- Plan and execute quantitative studies — surveys, unmoderated tests, A/B test analysis, analytics deep-dives
- Build and maintain a participant recruitment pipeline with diverse, representative sampling
- Synthesize research findings into actionable insights with clear recommendations and confidence levels
- Maintain a research repository — searchable, tagged, cumulative knowledge base of insights
- Present findings to stakeholders at every level — from design team to executive leadership
- Establish research operations — templates, tools, consent processes, incentive management, scheduling
- Train product teams on lightweight research methods they can run independently
- Define and track experience metrics — task success rate, NPS, CSAT, SUS, custom experience KPIs
- Conduct competitive research to understand market patterns and user expectations
- Advocate for underrepresented users and edge cases in product decisions

## Key Skills & Tools

### Qualitative Methods

| Method | Best For | Key Skills |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **User interviews** | Understanding motivations, mental models, decision-making processes | Semi-structured guide design, rapport building, probing techniques (5 Whys, tell-me-about-a-time), managing leading questions |
| **Contextual inquiry** | Understanding real-world context, workarounds, environmental factors | Field observation protocol, master-apprentice model, artifact analysis, environment documentation |
| **Diary studies** | Longitudinal behavior, infrequent events, emotional journeys over time | Study design (duration, prompt frequency), participant engagement, longitudinal analysis, attrition management |
| **Usability testing** | Identifying interaction problems, validating design solutions | Task design, think-aloud moderation, severity rating (Nielsen scale), highlight reels for stakeholders |
| **Concept testing** | Evaluating early ideas before committing to implementation | Stimulus design (low vs. high fidelity), desirability testing, reaction frameworks (Kano model) |
| **Focus groups** | Exploring attitudes, generating ideas (not for behavior or usability) | Facilitation, managing dominant voices, group dynamics awareness, knowing when NOT to use this method |
| **Card sorting** | Information architecture, categorization, labeling | Open/closed/hybrid sorting, analysis methods (similarity matrix, dendrograms), statistical interpretation |

### Quantitative Methods

| Method | Best For | Key Skills |
|--------|----------|------------|
| **Surveys** | Measuring attitudes, preferences, satisfaction at scale | Question design (avoiding bias, double-barreled questions), scale design (Likert, semantic differential), sampling strategy, statistical analysis |
| **A/B testing** | Measuring behavioral impact of design changes | Hypothesis formulation, sample size calculation, statistical significance, practical significance vs. p-values, guardrail metrics |
| **Analytics analysis** | Understanding actual user behavior patterns at scale | Funnel analysis, cohort analysis, segmentation, event tracking design, interpreting behavioral data |
| **Unmoderated testing** | Scalable usability evaluation with larger sample sizes | Task design for unmoderated context, quantitative usability metrics (completion rate, time-on-task, error rate), combining with qualitative follow-up |
| **Tree testing** | Validating information architecture findability | Task creation, success rate analysis, directness metrics, first-click analysis |
| **Benchmark studies** | Tracking experience quality over time | Standardized tasks, SUS/UMUX-Lite scoring, longitudinal comparison, regression analysis |

### Research Synthesis & Analysis

- **Affinity diagramming**: Clustering observations into themes — the foundational qualitative analysis technique
- **Thematic analysis**: Systematic coding of qualitative data to identify patterns, themes, and relationships
- **Jobs-to-be-done framework**: Structuring findings around functional, emotional, and social jobs users are trying to accomplish
- **Journey mapping**: Synthesizing research into end-to-end experience maps with pain points, opportunities, and emotional arcs
- **Persona development**: Evidence-based archetypes that represent distinct behavior patterns, goals, and contexts
- **Impact mapping**: Connecting research insights to product opportunities to business outcomes

### Research Operations

- **Participant recruitment**: Panel management (User Interviews, Respondent), screening criteria, diverse representation, incentive structures
- **Consent and ethics**: IRB-style consent processes, data privacy (GDPR-compliant research), secure data storage, participant wellbeing
- **Research repository**: Tagging taxonomy, searchable insight libraries (Dovetail, Notion, Airtable), preventing knowledge loss
- **Templates and playbooks**: Standardized study plans, interview guides, analysis frameworks, reporting templates
- **Research cadence**: Continuous discovery (Teresa Torres) — weekly touchpoints with users, not just project-based studies

## AI-First Practices

AI transforms the most time-consuming parts of research — analysis and synthesis — while the human researcher remains essential for study design, moderation, and interpretation:

- **Interview transcript analysis**: Feed interview transcripts to AI for initial coding — theme identification, sentiment patterns, quote extraction by topic. AI reduces synthesis time from days to hours. Always review and refine AI-generated codes against your theoretical framework
- **Survey design assistance**: Describe your research questions to AI to generate initial survey drafts with appropriate question types, scale designs, and flow logic. AI catches common survey design errors (double-barreled questions, leading phrasing, missing options)
- **Competitive research synthesis**: Use AI to analyze competitor products, reviews, and support forums to identify UX patterns, common user complaints, and unmet needs across the market
- **Insight pattern recognition**: Feed your research repository data to AI to surface cross-study patterns — recurring themes, contradictions between studies, and gaps in your research coverage
- **Research plan generation**: Describe your research question and constraints (timeline, budget, participant access) to AI to generate study plan drafts — method selection, participant criteria, discussion guide structure
- **Highlight reel creation**: Use AI to identify key moments in session recordings based on transcript analysis — participant frustration, delight, confusion, or insight moments
- **Report writing**: Use AI to draft research reports from your coded data and analysis notes. AI handles structure and prose while you ensure the insights and recommendations are accurate

### AI Anti-Patterns in UX Research
- Replacing user interviews with AI-simulated users — AI cannot replicate real human behavior, emotions, or context
- Using AI to generate "user insights" without actual user data — this is fabrication, not research
- Trusting AI coding of qualitative data without researcher review — AI misses nuance, sarcasm, and cultural context
- Automating research so completely that researchers lose direct contact with participants — empathy requires exposure

## How They Collaborate

- **UX Designers**: Co-define research questions, share findings that directly inform design decisions, collaborate on usability test design, validate design direction through research
- **Product Managers**: Align research roadmap with product strategy, provide evidence for prioritization decisions, challenge assumptions with data, define success metrics together
- **UI Designers**: Include visual design variables in usability studies, test brand perception and aesthetic preferences, evaluate design system comprehension
- **Data Analysts/Scientists**: Triangulate qualitative findings with quantitative behavioral data, collaborate on A/B test design and interpretation, share segmentation insights
- **Engineering**: Share technical feasibility constraints that shape research scope, include engineers in user sessions for firsthand empathy exposure
- **Customer Success/Support**: Access customer feedback themes, coordinate research to avoid survey fatigue, share support ticket analysis
- **Executive Leadership**: Present strategic research findings that inform product direction, build executive empathy for user needs through compelling storytelling

## Hiring Signals

### Green Flags
- Can articulate why they chose a specific method for a specific question — not "we always do usability testing"
- Shows a portfolio with both qualitative and quantitative methods — not over-indexed on either
- Demonstrates synthesis skill — can explain how they went from raw data to actionable insights
- Has experience with participant recruitment and understands sampling bias
- Shows research that changed a product decision — with evidence of impact, not just delivery
- Asks good questions in the interview itself — curious, open-ended, probing without leading
- Understands research ethics — consent, privacy, participant wellbeing, data handling
- Can explain statistical concepts relevant to their quantitative work (significance, sample size, confidence intervals)
- Has experience building or contributing to a research repository

### Red Flags
- Only does one type of research (typically usability testing) for every question
- Cannot explain how their research influenced a product decision — research as theater
- "Users said they want X, so we should build X" — taking stated preferences at face value without triangulation
- No systematic analysis method — "I just read through the transcripts and picked out quotes"
- Cannot discuss recruitment strategy, sampling, or participant diversity
- Presents findings without confidence levels or limitations — treats all findings as equally certain
- Research reports are 80+ slides with no clear recommendations or decision framework
- Has never worked with quantitative data or dismisses it as "not real research"
- Cannot explain the difference between generative and evaluative research

## Growth Path

| Level | Markers |
|-------|---------|
| **Junior** | Conducts usability tests and interviews with guidance. Takes detailed notes and assists with synthesis. Manages participant scheduling and incentives. Follows established research templates and processes. Creates highlight clips from sessions |
| **Mid** | Plans and executes research studies independently — method selection, participant criteria, guide design, analysis, reporting. Synthesizes findings into actionable recommendations. Manages a participant panel. Presents findings to product teams. Conducts both qualitative and quantitative studies |
| **Senior** | Owns research strategy for a product area. Designs mixed-methods studies that triangulate across data sources. Builds research operations (templates, repositories, processes). Mentors junior researchers and coaches non-researchers on lightweight methods. Influences product strategy with cumulative research insights. Establishes experience metrics and benchmarks |
| **Staff** | Defines organization-wide research strategy and methodology standards. Architects the research repository and knowledge management system. Builds research democratization programs — enabling product teams to run their own research with quality guardrails. Drives cross-product insight synthesis that surfaces strategic patterns. Establishes research ethics and governance. Connects research practice to business outcomes with clear measurement |
| **Principal** | Sets the research vision across the organization. Defines how user evidence integrates into strategic decision-making. Builds research as an organizational capability — team structure, career paths, tool infrastructure. Drives industry contributions through publications, conference talks, and methodology innovation. Advises executive leadership on user-centered strategy. Evaluates emerging research methods and technologies (AI-assisted research, behavioral data integration) |

## RTFM — Essential Reading

### Books
- [Just Enough Research](https://abookapart.com/products/just-enough-research) — Erika Hall. The most practical, no-nonsense research book. Covers when and how to do research with real-world constraints. Start here
- [Observing the User Experience](https://www.elsevier.com/books/observing-the-user-experience/goodman/978-0-12-384869-7) — Elizabeth Goodman, Mike Kuniavsky, Andrea Moed. Comprehensive practitioner's guide covering the full range of UX research methods
- [Interviewing Users](https://rosenfeldmedia.com/books/interviewing-users/) — Steve Portigal. The definitive guide to conducting effective user interviews — rapport, questioning techniques, analysis, and the art of listening
- [Quantifying the User Experience](https://www.elsevier.com/books/quantifying-the-user-experience/sauro/978-0-12-802308-2) — Jeff Sauro & James Lewis. Statistical methods for UX research — sample sizes, confidence intervals, task metrics, and survey analysis
- [Continuous Discovery Habits](https://www.producttalkgroup.com/continuous-discovery-habits) — Teresa Torres. Building weekly touchpoints with users into the product development process — research as a continuous activity, not a project phase
- [Practical Empathy](https://rosenfeldmedia.com/books/practical-empathy/) — Indi Young. Understanding people's motivations and mental models through listening sessions and mental model diagrams
- [Research That Scales](https://www.userinterviews.com/research-that-scales) — Kate Towsey. Building research operations that enable research impact at organizational scale
