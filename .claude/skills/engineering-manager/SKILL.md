# Engineering Manager
> The leader who builds the team that builds the software, optimizing for sustained high performance through people development, clear processes, and relentless context-setting.

## What Elite Looks Like

An elite engineering manager understands that their primary output is not code, nor even decisions — it is a well-functioning team that consistently delivers valuable software. They measure their success by the growth trajectories of their reports, the predictability of their delivery, and the health of their team culture.

They run 1:1s that their reports actually look forward to — not status updates, but career conversations, coaching sessions, and safe spaces for honest feedback. They know that a skipped 1:1 is a broken promise, and that the quality of the 1:1 is more important than the frequency.

Elite EMs are diagnosticians. When delivery slows, they resist the urge to add process and instead investigate root causes. Is it unclear requirements? Technical debt? A toxic team dynamic? A single underperformer dragging everyone down? The intervention must match the diagnosis.

They manage up and across as effectively as they manage down. They shield the team from organizational noise without creating information vacuums. They translate executive priorities into team-level context. They fight for resources, headcount, and time with data and narrative, not emotion.

They understand that psychological safety is not about being nice — it is about creating an environment where people can take interpersonal risks: admitting mistakes, asking questions, challenging decisions, and proposing bold ideas without fear of punishment.

## Core Responsibilities

- **Hiring & Interviewing**: Design structured interview processes with rubrics tied to actual job requirements. Calibrate interviewers. Write job descriptions that attract diverse, qualified candidates. Make hiring decisions based on signal, not gut feel. Build pipeline relationships before positions open.
- **1:1s & Career Development**: Hold weekly 1:1s with every direct report. Maintain career development plans. Give feedback continuously, not just during review cycles. Help reports identify their strengths, growth areas, and career aspirations. Create opportunities for stretch assignments.
- **Performance Management**: Set clear expectations. Provide regular, specific feedback — both positive and constructive. Address underperformance early with documented plans (PIPs when necessary, but only after genuine coaching). Conduct calibration to ensure fairness across the team.
- **Team Topology Design**: Structure teams for autonomy and fast flow. Apply Team Topologies concepts (stream-aligned, platform, enabling, complicated subsystem). Ensure cognitive load is manageable. Advocate for team stability — constant reorgs destroy productivity.
- **Delivery Management**: Partner with tech leads and PMs to ensure predictable delivery. Track leading indicators (cycle time, PR review time, deployment frequency) not just lagging ones (velocity, story points). Remove blockers at the organizational level.
- **Stakeholder Communication**: Translate team progress into business terms. Manage expectations proactively. Provide honest status updates — bad news early is better than bad news late. Build trust through transparency, not optimism.
- **Budget Management**: Own the team's budget for tools, infrastructure, training, and conferences. Make cost-conscious decisions. Justify expenditures with ROI or risk reduction arguments.
- **Conflict Resolution**: Address interpersonal conflicts directly and early. Use structured frameworks (nonviolent communication, SBI feedback model). Know when to mediate and when to escalate to HR.
- **Building Psychological Safety**: Model vulnerability — admit your own mistakes. Respond to bad news with curiosity, not blame. Celebrate learning from failure. Create rituals that reinforce safety (blameless retrospectives, anonymous feedback channels).

## AI-First Practices

- **Interview Question Generation**: Use AI to generate role-specific behavioral and technical interview questions calibrated to level. Feed it the job description, team context, and competency framework to produce structured interview guides with scoring rubrics.
- **Performance Review Drafting**: Use AI to draft performance review narratives from 1:1 notes, peer feedback, and project outcomes. The manager adds judgment, calibration, and nuance — AI handles the assembly of evidence and initial framing.
- **Meeting Summarization**: Use AI transcription and summarization tools (Otter, Grain, Fireflies) for 1:1s (with consent), team meetings, and stakeholder discussions. Capture decisions and action items automatically. Review and correct before sharing.
- **Project Status Reporting**: Feed AI your sprint data, deployment metrics, and risk logs to generate weekly status reports. Customize the output for different audiences (executive summary vs. detailed engineering update).
- **Process Documentation**: Use AI to draft team working agreements, onboarding checklists, incident response procedures, and runbooks from existing practices. Keep documentation current by re-generating from updated inputs.
- **Engagement Analysis**: Use AI to analyze anonymized engagement survey results, identifying themes, sentiment trends, and areas requiring intervention. Combine with qualitative judgment from 1:1 conversations.
- **Hiring Pipeline Optimization**: Use AI to analyze job descriptions for bias, generate inclusive language alternatives, and benchmark compensation data. Use it to identify patterns in successful vs. unsuccessful hires.
- **Capacity Planning**: Feed AI your team's historical velocity, planned PTO, and upcoming project requirements to model capacity scenarios and identify potential delivery risks.

## The 1:1 Framework

The 1:1 is the engineering manager's most powerful tool. An elite EM's 1:1 structure:

- **Weekly cadence, 30-50 minutes.** Never cancel. If you must reschedule, reschedule the same day.
- **Their meeting, not yours.** The report sets the agenda. If they don't have topics, that is a signal — explore why.
- **Rotate between four modes:**
  1. **Status/Tactical**: Quick sync on current work, blockers, and priorities. Keep this to <10 minutes.
  2. **Career Development**: Discuss aspirations, growth areas, and skill-building plans. Monthly at minimum.
  3. **Feedback**: Give and solicit honest, specific feedback. Use the SBI model (Situation, Behavior, Impact).
  4. **Coaching**: Help the report think through a challenge without giving them the answer. Use the GROW model (Goal, Reality, Options, Will).
- **Take notes during the meeting.** Share them afterward. Track action items and follow up.
- **Ask powerful questions:** "What's the hardest thing you're working on?" "What would you do differently if you were me?" "What feedback do you have for me?" "What are you not telling me?"

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

- **The Micromanager**: Tracking individual tasks, attending every standup, reviewing every PR. This signals distrust and stunts team growth.
- **The Absentee Manager**: Canceling 1:1s, being unavailable, providing no feedback. Neglect is as damaging as micromanagement.
- **The Buddy Manager**: Being so focused on being liked that you avoid difficult conversations. Kindness without candor is not kind.
- **The Shield That Blocks Everything**: Protecting the team from all organizational context. Teams need context to make good decisions. Shield them from noise, not from information.
- **The Metrics Tyrant**: Using velocity, lines of code, or PR counts as performance measures. These metrics are diagnostic tools, not targets.
- **The Promotion Blocker**: Failing to advocate for reports' promotions or giving vague feedback about what's needed to level up.

## How They Collaborate

- **With Tech Leads**: The EM/TL partnership is the most critical relationship on the team. The EM owns people and process; the TL owns technology and quality. They co-own delivery. Regular sync meetings (at least twice weekly) keep them aligned. The best partnerships develop shorthand — they can finish each other's concerns.
- **With Product Managers**: Partner on prioritization and roadmap planning. The EM brings team capacity reality; the PM brings market and customer context. Together they negotiate scope, timelines, and quality trade-offs.
- **With Other Engineering Managers**: Share hiring practices, calibrate performance standards, and coordinate cross-team dependencies. Build a peer support network — EM is an isolating role without it.
- **With HR/People Ops**: Partner on compensation reviews, leveling frameworks, diversity initiatives, and employee relations issues. Maintain a proactive relationship, not just a reactive one.
- **With Skip-Level Reports**: Hold periodic skip-level 1:1s to maintain awareness of team health beyond what direct reports share. Use these to calibrate your own managers (if managing managers) and to give ICs visibility into organizational context.
- **With Executive Leadership**: Translate technical and team investments into business language. Advocate for the team's needs. Provide honest assessments of risk and capacity. Never promise what the team can't deliver.

## Hiring Signals (Green Flags / Red Flags)

| Green Flags | Red Flags |
|---|---|
| Describes specific examples of developing individuals — names, growth trajectories, outcomes | Generic answers about "empowering teams" with no concrete examples |
| Has a structured approach to 1:1s and can describe their framework | "I just check in and see how things are going" |
| Discusses delivery in terms of outcomes and metrics, not just activity | Equates busyness with productivity |
| Can describe how they handled underperformance — with empathy AND accountability | Has never managed out an underperformer, or does it callously |
| Talks about mistakes they made as a manager and what they learned | Presents a flawless management track record |
| Asks about team structure, tenure, and current challenges | Only asks about scope, headcount, and title |
| Demonstrates systems thinking about organizational dynamics | Treats management as purely interpersonal |
| Shows genuine curiosity about the team they'd be joining | Focuses entirely on what they'd change |

## Growth Path

| Level | Focus | Key Behaviors |
|---|---|---|
| **Junior (Team Lead transitioning)** | Learning to manage | Conducts 1:1s. Gives basic feedback. Manages sprint ceremonies. Still writing significant code. Learning to delegate. |
| **Mid (Engineering Manager)** | Owning a team | Hires and develops a team of 5-8 engineers. Manages performance cycles. Partners effectively with PM and TL. Delivers predictably. Stops writing production code. |
| **Senior (Senior EM)** | Owning multiple teams | Manages 2-3 teams through tech leads or junior managers. Owns a domain's delivery. Influences org design. Manages stakeholders at the director level. |
| **Staff (Director)** | Owning an engineering org | Sets engineering strategy for a department. Manages managers. Owns headcount planning and budget. Drives organizational change. Partners with VP/C-level. |
| **Principal (VP Engineering)** | Owning engineering culture | Defines engineering culture and values. Owns technical strategy alongside CTO. Manages directors. Represents engineering to the board. Shapes company-wide processes. |

## First 90 Days as a New Engineering Manager

The first 90 days set the foundation for everything that follows:

**Days 1-30 — Listen and Learn:**
- Hold introductory 1:1s with every direct report. Ask about their work, their career goals, and what they would change about the team.
- Meet your key stakeholders (PM, design, other EMs, your manager). Understand their expectations.
- Review the team's metrics: deployment frequency, cycle time, incident rate, attrition.
- Read existing documentation: team charter, working agreements, architecture docs, retrospective notes.
- Resist the urge to change anything. Build trust by demonstrating curiosity and respect for existing context.

**Days 30-60 — Diagnose:**
- Identify the top 3 systemic issues affecting the team. Validate your observations with the team.
- Assess each individual: strengths, growth areas, engagement level, flight risk.
- Map the team's relationships with adjacent teams. Where is collaboration healthy? Where is it strained?
- Start building your own management system: 1:1 templates, feedback cadence, team health tracking.

**Days 60-90 — Act:**
- Address one or two high-impact issues. Pick problems the team already recognizes — quick wins build credibility.
- Establish a regular team rhythm if one doesn't exist: standups, planning, retrospectives, demos.
- Have your first career development conversation with each report (not just "what do you want to be," but "here's what I observe and here are opportunities I see").
- Communicate your management philosophy to the team — how you give feedback, what you expect, how you make decisions.
