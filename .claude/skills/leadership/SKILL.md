# Leadership
> The founder who builds the machine that builds the company — attracting, developing, and retaining exceptional people through clarity of vision, culture by design, and the discipline to make hard decisions.

## What Elite Looks Like

An elite founder-level leader understands that their primary job shifts over time: from doing the work, to hiring people who do the work, to building the organization that enables people to do their best work. Garry Tan's insight is precise — "the broader you understand these areas, the more high-quality people you'll attract." Exceptional engineers, designers, and operators want to work for founders who understand what great looks like across every function. Leadership is the meta-skill that multiplies all other founder skills.

What separates elite from competent:
- **Talent magnetism**: The best people want to work with them — not because of compensation alone, but because of the mission, the culture, and the quality of thinking. They recruit before they have open roles
- **Culture as operating system**: Doesn't leave culture to chance. Explicitly designs values, norms, and rituals that reinforce the behaviors the company needs. Culture is how people behave when the founder isn't in the room
- **Decision velocity**: Makes decisions at the right speed — fast on reversible decisions (Type 2), deliberate on irreversible ones (Type 1). Never lets indecision become the default
- **Communication clarity**: Can explain complex strategy to any audience — board, engineers, customers, press — in language they understand. Writes clearly. Speaks with conviction but not arrogance
- **Delegation with accountability**: Hires people better than themselves in each function, gives them context and authority, then holds them accountable to outcomes — not activities
- **Emotional regulation**: Stays calm in crises. Doesn't let personal stress infect the team. Manages their own psychology as seriously as they manage the business

## Core Responsibilities

- Define company mission, vision, and values — and ensure they're lived, not laminated
- Recruit, hire, and retain exceptional talent across all functions
- Design the organizational structure and evolve it as the company scales
- Make critical decisions: strategy, resource allocation, hiring/firing, partnerships, pivots
- Communicate vision and strategy to all stakeholders: team, board, investors, customers, press
- Build and manage the executive team and leadership bench
- Set the cadence: weekly team meetings, quarterly planning, annual strategy, board meetings
- Navigate conflict: interpersonal, strategic, and cultural disagreements
- Manage the board of directors: prepare meetings, maintain relationships, leverage expertise
- Model the culture: your behavior sets the ceiling for everyone else's

## Key Skills & Tools

### Hiring & Recruiting
- Job architecture: defining roles, levels, and career ladders before hiring
- Structured interviewing: scorecards, calibrated rubrics, interview loops that reduce bias
- Sourcing: building a talent pipeline before you need it — conferences, communities, personal network
- Closing: selling the vision, handling competing offers, structuring compelling equity packages
- Reference checking: the art of the back-channel reference (more signal than formal references)
- Onboarding: first 90 days plan that sets new hires up for success

### Culture Building
| Element | Implementation |
|---------|---------------|
| **Values** | 3-5 specific, actionable principles that guide real decisions. Not generic ("integrity") but distinctive ("move fast and break things" or "disagree and commit") |
| **Rituals** | Weekly all-hands, demo days, retrospectives, team dinners — predictable moments that reinforce culture |
| **Norms** | How decisions are made, how meetings are run, how feedback is given, how conflict is handled |
| **Documentation** | Written culture handbooks, decision logs, strategy memos — culture scales through writing |
| **Recognition** | Public celebration of behaviors that exemplify values — not just outcomes, but how they were achieved |

### Decision-Making Frameworks
- Type 1 vs. Type 2 decisions (Bezos): irreversible decisions get deliberation; reversible ones get speed
- DACI framework: Driver, Approver, Contributors, Informed — clarifies who decides
- Pre-mortem analysis: "Imagine this decision failed. Why?" before committing
- Decision journaling: record the decision, the reasoning, and the information available — review later for calibration
- Disagree and commit: healthy debate followed by unified execution

### Communication
- Written communication: strategy memos, investor updates, company announcements, all-hands presentations
- Verbal communication: 1:1s, team meetings, board presentations, conference keynotes, media interviews
- Difficult conversations: delivering hard feedback, managing underperformance, letting people go
- Narrative structure: situation → complication → resolution framework for strategic communication
- Active listening: synthesizing what you hear, asking clarifying questions, making people feel heard

### Organizational Design
- Org structure: functional, divisional, matrix — choosing the right structure for each growth stage
- Span of control: how many direct reports per manager (typically 5-8)
- Two-pizza teams: small autonomous teams with clear ownership
- Interface design: how teams communicate and coordinate (APIs between teams, not shared resources)
- Scaling culture: how to maintain culture through 10→50→200→1000 employee transitions

### Board Management
- Board composition: choosing board members who add strategic value, not just capital
- Board meeting preparation: executive summary, key metrics, strategic questions, ask
- Board relationships: maintaining trust through transparency, especially when things are hard
- Leveraging board expertise: using board members as advisors, connectors, and sounding boards

## AI-First Practices

AI is transforming how leaders communicate, organize, and make decisions. The elite founder uses AI to amplify their leadership capacity — not to replace the human connection that leadership fundamentally requires.

### Hiring & Talent
- AI-generated job descriptions that are specific, inclusive, and compelling — starting from role requirements and company voice
- Resume screening assistance: AI identifies patterns in successful hires and flags promising candidates
- Interview question generation tailored to specific competencies and seniority levels
- Offer letter and compensation analysis: AI benchmarks against market data

### Communication & Documentation
- Meeting summarization and action item extraction from recorded meetings (Otter.ai, Fireflies, Granola)
- Strategy document drafting: use AI to create first drafts of company memos, quarterly plans, and board decks from bullet points
- All-hands presentation generation: transform raw updates into narrative-structured presentations
- Investor update drafting: consistent, professional monthly updates from raw metrics and notes

### Organizational Design & Analysis
- AI-assisted org design: model different structures and identify potential communication bottlenecks
- Team health analysis: synthesize engagement survey data, 1:1 notes, and retention patterns into actionable insights
- Succession planning: AI-assisted gap analysis of leadership bench strength

### Decision Support
- Decision brief generation: AI synthesizes relevant data, precedents, and risk factors for major decisions
- Scenario modeling for organizational changes: "If we restructure engineering into squads, what are the likely communication and coordination impacts?"
- Historical decision analysis: review past decisions and outcomes to improve future calibration

## How They Collaborate

- **All Functions**: Leadership is the connective tissue. The founder-leader ensures alignment across sales, product, engineering, brand, and finance through clear communication of priorities and strategy
- **Sales & Marketing**: Hires and enables the GTM team. Participates in key customer and partner relationships. Sets revenue expectations aligned with the financial plan
- **Product & Engineering**: Sets the product vision. Hires technical leadership. Balances speed and quality. Makes tie-breaking decisions on roadmap priorities
- **Finance**: Partners on fundraising. Manages board relationships. Sets hiring plan within budget constraints. Makes resource allocation decisions
- **Brand**: Is the primary brand ambassador. Personal brand and company brand are intertwined. Sets the tone for all external communications
- **Board**: Manages up: keeps the board informed, aligned, and supportive. Knows when to ask for help and when to lead

## Hiring Signals

### Green Flags
- People from their previous companies want to work with them again — strong back-channel references
- Can articulate their management philosophy with specific examples, not just platitudes
- Has made hard decisions (letting people go, killing projects, pivoting) and can discuss them with empathy and clarity
- Demonstrates self-awareness: knows their strengths, weaknesses, and blind spots — and has systems to compensate
- Shows genuine curiosity about people: asks questions, remembers details, follows up
- Has built a team or organization from scratch — not just managed an existing one
- Can point to people they've mentored who have gone on to leadership roles themselves

### Red Flags
- "I'm a great leader because I work harder than everyone" — confuses output with leadership
- Can't name specific people they've developed or teams they've built
- Avoids difficult conversations: "Everyone on my team is great" with no nuance
- Over-indexes on command-and-control: micromanages decisions that should be delegated
- No self-awareness about failures or mistakes — only success stories
- Treats hiring as a transactional process rather than a relationship-building one
- Can't articulate company values or culture beyond generic platitudes
- "I don't need a board — they just slow us down" — adversarial relationship with governance

## Growth Path

| Level | Markers |
|-------|---------|
| **Solo Founder** | Leads by doing everything. Recruits co-founder and first employees through personal conviction. Makes all decisions. Culture is implicit — it's just "how we do things" |
| **Founder (5-15 people)** | First real management challenges. Hires first team leads. Must learn to delegate and trust. Codifies initial values and cultural norms. Runs first structured hiring process |
| **Founder-CEO (15-50 people)** | Builds executive team. Establishes organizational structure. Implements management cadence (1:1s, team meetings, all-hands). First board management. Culture must be explicitly designed |
| **CEO (50-200 people)** | Leads through executives, not individual contributors. Manages complex org dynamics. Navigates first serious cultural challenges. Board becomes a real governance body |
| **CEO (200+ people)** | Fully organizational leader. Sets strategy, culture, and vision. Hires and develops C-suite. Manages board and external stakeholders. Personal decisions have company-wide ripple effects |
