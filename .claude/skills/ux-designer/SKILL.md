# UX Designer

> The designer who makes complex systems feel simple — translating user needs, business goals, and technical constraints into interactions that are intuitive, accessible, and measurably effective.

## What Elite Looks Like

A staff-level UX designer doesn't just create wireframes and prototypes. They own the end-to-end experience strategy — defining how users think, navigate, and accomplish their goals within a product. They bring research-backed rigor to every design decision, advocate fiercely for the user while respecting technical and business constraints, and establish design patterns that scale across teams and product surfaces.

What separates elite from competent:
- **Systems-level thinking**: Designs coherent experiences across entire product ecosystems, not just individual screens. Understands how a change in one flow affects five others
- **Research-driven intuition**: Has done enough user research that their "gut feelings" are actually pattern recognition from hundreds of user sessions — and they can articulate the evidence behind their instincts
- **Constraint navigation**: Treats technical limitations and business requirements as creative constraints, not obstacles. Finds elegant solutions within real-world boundaries
- **Accessibility as default**: Doesn't bolt on accessibility at the end — designs for diverse abilities, contexts, and devices from the start (WCAG AA minimum, AAA where possible)
- **Measurable outcomes**: Ties design decisions to metrics — task completion rates, time-on-task, error rates, NPS/CSAT — not just stakeholder opinions or aesthetic preference
- **Facilitation mastery**: Leads design thinking workshops, stakeholder alignment sessions, and critique sessions that generate clarity from ambiguity
- **Handoff excellence**: Delivers specifications so clear that engineers never have to guess intent — annotated prototypes, interaction specs, edge case documentation, and responsive behavior definitions

## Core Responsibilities

- Conduct and synthesize user research to inform design decisions (interviews, usability tests, contextual inquiry)
- Define information architecture — navigation structures, content hierarchy, taxonomies, and mental model alignment
- Create user flows, journey maps, and task analyses for critical user paths
- Design wireframes and interactive prototypes that communicate behavior, not just layout
- Conduct usability testing to validate design hypotheses and identify friction points
- Establish and maintain interaction patterns that create consistency across the product
- Design for accessibility (WCAG 2.2 AA) across all features and platforms
- Collaborate with UI designers on visual execution and with engineers on technical feasibility
- Present and defend design decisions to stakeholders with evidence-based rationale
- Map and optimize cross-channel user journeys (web, mobile, email, support)
- Define micro-interactions and error states that guide users through edge cases gracefully
- Mentor junior designers on UX methodology and craft

## Key Skills & Tools

### Research & Discovery

| Method | When to Use | Key Skills |
|--------|------------|------------|
| **User interviews** | Understanding motivations, mental models, pain points | Interview guide design, active listening, probing techniques, synthesis |
| **Contextual inquiry** | Understanding real-world usage context and workarounds | Field observation, think-aloud protocol, artifact analysis |
| **Usability testing** | Validating design decisions against real user behavior | Task design, think-aloud moderation, severity rating, insight extraction |
| **Card sorting** | Defining information architecture and navigation | Open vs. closed sorting, hybrid methods, dendogram analysis |
| **Tree testing** | Validating navigation structure before visual design | Task creation, success rate analysis, directness metrics |
| **Heuristic evaluation** | Quick identification of usability issues against established principles | Nielsen's heuristics, severity rating, prioritization |
| **Journey mapping** | Understanding end-to-end experience across touchpoints | Touchpoint identification, emotional mapping, opportunity identification |
| **Competitive analysis** | Understanding market patterns and opportunities for differentiation | Feature matrices, UX pattern comparison, gap analysis |

### Information Architecture

- **Navigation design**: Top-level structure, wayfinding, breadcrumbs, contextual navigation
- **Content hierarchy**: Information scent, progressive disclosure, chunking, scannable layouts
- **Mental model alignment**: Matching system structure to how users think about tasks and information
- **Taxonomy design**: Labeling systems, categorization schemes, search and filtering architecture
- **Sitemap creation**: Structural documentation of content and feature organization

### Interaction Design

- **User flows**: Task-based flow diagrams that document every path, decision point, and error state
- **Wireframing**: Low-fidelity structure and layout exploration (Figma, Balsamiq)
- **Prototyping**: Interactive prototypes that communicate behavior — transitions, conditional logic, micro-interactions (Figma prototyping, Framer)
- **Form design**: Input patterns, validation strategies, error messaging, progressive disclosure for complex forms
- **Error handling**: Designing for failure — error prevention, clear error messages, recovery paths
- **Empty states**: First-run experiences, zero-data states, error states that guide users forward
- **Responsive patterns**: Designing adaptive layouts that work across screen sizes without losing functionality or usability

### Design Thinking & Facilitation

- **Design sprints**: Structured 5-day process for solving big problems and testing ideas (GV Design Sprint)
- **Workshop facilitation**: How Might We exercises, affinity diagramming, dot voting, design studio
- **Stakeholder alignment**: Design critiques, decision frameworks, RACI for design decisions
- **Design principles**: Defining and applying product-specific design principles that guide decision-making

### Accessibility (WCAG 2.2)

- **Perceivable**: Text alternatives, captions, adaptable content, distinguishable contrast
- **Operable**: Keyboard accessibility, timing, seizure prevention, navigability, input modalities
- **Understandable**: Readable content, predictable behavior, input assistance
- **Robust**: Compatible with assistive technologies, valid markup, ARIA when needed

### Tools

- **Figma**: Component-based design, auto-layout, prototyping, design tokens, dev mode for handoff
- **FigJam / Miro**: Workshop facilitation, journey mapping, affinity diagramming, collaborative ideation
- **Maze / UserTesting**: Remote unmoderated usability testing, task analysis, heatmaps
- **Optimal Workshop**: Card sorting, tree testing, first-click testing
- **Hotjar / FullStory**: Session recordings, heatmaps, and behavioral analytics for understanding real usage patterns

## AI-First Practices

AI amplifies UX design by accelerating research synthesis, ideation, and validation:

- **Rapid prototyping**: Describe user flows and interface requirements to AI to generate initial wireframe concepts and interaction patterns. Use as starting points for iteration, not final designs
- **User flow generation**: Feed user stories or job-to-be-done descriptions to AI to map out comprehensive user flows including edge cases, error states, and alternative paths you might not have considered
- **Competitive analysis acceleration**: Use AI to analyze competitor products, synthesize UX patterns, and identify differentiating opportunities. AI excels at pattern recognition across large sets of competitive data
- **Usability heuristic checks**: Describe your interface or share screenshots with AI to get a systematic heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's 10 heuristics. Use as a supplement to — never a replacement for — actual usability testing with real users
- **Accessibility audit assistance**: Feed design specifications to AI for preliminary WCAG compliance checks — contrast ratios, heading hierarchy, alternative text requirements, keyboard interaction patterns
- **Copy and microcopy generation**: Use AI to draft interface text — button labels, error messages, onboarding copy, tooltip content — then refine for voice, tone, and brevity
- **Research synthesis**: Feed interview transcripts and usability test notes to AI for pattern identification, theme clustering, and insight extraction. AI dramatically reduces synthesis time for qualitative data
- **User persona development**: Provide research data to AI to draft persona documents — behaviors, goals, pain points, and scenarios. Always validate against real research data

### AI Anti-Patterns in UX Design
- Replacing user research with AI assumptions about user behavior — AI doesn't know your users
- Accepting AI-generated flows without validating against real mental models through testing
- Using AI to design for "average users" — accessibility and edge cases get lost
- Treating AI critique as equivalent to usability testing with real participants

## How They Collaborate

- **UI Designers**: Co-define the visual execution of interaction patterns, ensure visual design serves usability goals
- **UX Researchers**: Commission research studies, co-analyze findings, translate insights into design direction
- **Product Managers**: Align user needs with business goals, co-prioritize features based on user impact, negotiate scope
- **Frontend Engineers**: Collaborate on technical feasibility, define interactive behavior, ensure accessibility implementation
- **Content Designers/Writers**: Co-design information hierarchy, interface copy, and content strategy
- **Data Analysts**: Define success metrics for design changes, analyze behavioral data, set up A/B test parameters
- **Engineering Managers**: Negotiate design quality vs. delivery timeline tradeoffs, advocate for UX debt remediation

## Hiring Signals

### Green Flags
- Shows a portfolio that explains the problem, process, and outcome — not just pretty screens
- Can articulate design decisions with user research evidence, not just personal preference
- Demonstrates accessibility knowledge and integrates it naturally into their process
- Asks clarifying questions about users, context, and constraints before jumping to solutions
- Has conducted real usability tests and can describe surprising findings that changed their design
- Shows information architecture thinking — navigation design, content hierarchy, user mental models
- Can present multiple design directions with tradeoff analysis, not just one solution
- Handles critique gracefully — separates ego from work, asks clarifying questions, iterates constructively

### Red Flags
- Portfolio is all visual design with no explanation of user problems or research
- "I just know what users want" without evidence or research methodology
- Designs only for the happy path — no error states, empty states, or edge cases
- Cannot explain WCAG basics or has never designed for accessibility
- Presents one solution as the solution — no exploration of alternatives or tradeoffs
- Does not mention usability testing or any form of design validation
- Cannot articulate how they hand off to engineering — "they just look at the Figma file"
- Gets defensive about design feedback rather than treating it as useful signal

## Growth Path

| Level | Markers |
|-------|---------|
| **Junior** | Creates wireframes and prototypes from defined requirements. Conducts usability tests with guidance. Follows established design patterns and accessibility guidelines. Documents interaction specifications for straightforward flows |
| **Mid** | Independently defines user flows and information architecture for features. Conducts user research (interviews, usability tests) and synthesizes findings. Designs for edge cases and error states. Collaborates effectively with engineers on implementation. Contributes to the design system |
| **Senior** | Owns UX strategy for a product area. Leads research planning and synthesis. Designs complex multi-step workflows and cross-platform experiences. Mentors junior designers. Establishes interaction patterns adopted across the product. Facilitates design workshops and stakeholder alignment sessions. Advocates for accessibility and inclusive design |
| **Staff** | Defines product-wide experience strategy and design principles. Architects information architecture across product surfaces. Establishes UX quality standards and review processes. Drives cross-functional alignment on experience priorities. Introduces new UX methodologies and tools to the organization. Connects UX metrics to business outcomes. Mentors senior designers |
| **Principal** | Sets UX vision across the organization. Defines how the company thinks about user experience as a competitive advantage. Establishes UX research and design operations at scale. Drives organizational structure decisions (centralized vs. embedded design). Represents user experience in executive strategy discussions. Influences industry through speaking, writing, and community leadership |
